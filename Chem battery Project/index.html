The Chemical History of Batteries

"Voltaic Pile" - The first chemical battery

Created by Luigi Galvani, Alessandro Volta the voltaic pile was the first chemical battery. A voltaic pile consists of copper ( cathode ) and zinc ( anode ) disks separated by a cloth dipped in brine (a salt solution).


Zn half reaction:
Zn(s) -> Zn+2 (aq) + 2e-

Cu half reaction:
Cu+2(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s)

The net cell reaction is:
Zn(s) + Cu+2(aq) -> Zn+2(aq) + Cu(s)

If you were to connect a piece of zinc and copper without an electrolyte the current would flow momentarily. After a few moments, however electrons would build up at the cathode (copper plate) preventing any further flow of electrons. In textbooks, the electrode and electrolyte are often the same element for ease of comprehension. Fortunately you don't need the same elements as long as a reaction occurs. In the absence of a salt bridge the voltaic pile uses a cloth dipped in brine.

One of the negatively charged chlorine ions gives up an electron to the positively charged zinc. The neutral chlorine atom then bonds with another neutral chlorine atom turning into chlorine gas and evaporating.

Although the most obvious next reaction would be the sodium gaining an electron from the negatively charged copper this is not the case. Sodium’s low electronegativity means that there is not enough energy for it to gain another electron. Instead the water molecules on the surface of the copper react. The water molecule accepts an electron from the copper producing hydroxide and a hydrogen atom. The hydroxide remains inside the solution while the hydrogen bonds with another neutral hydrogen forming hydrogen gas and evaporating. Now the electron buildup is overcome and electrons can continue to flow.


Cl half reaction(s) at anode:
Cl-1 -> Cl + electron (to the zinc)
Cl + Cl -> Cl2

H20 half reaction at cathode:
H2O + electron (from copper) -> OH- + H2

The net electrolyte reaction is:
2NaCl (aq) + 2H2O -> 2NaOH (aq) + H2 + Cl2

"Daniell Cell"- Introduction to practical batteries

With the introduction of the Daniell cell came visions of batteries’ practical use. Improving upon many of the voltaic pile’s flaws the Daniell cell saw popularity in telegraph networks. The battery was so impressive that the electromotive force of the Daniell cell was used to define the volt ( with later definitions specifying electromotive force at 25° the cell now produces 1.10V ).


Zn/ZnSO4 half reaction ( oxidation )
Zn(s) -> Zn2+(aq) + 2e− ( E°: −0.7618 V )

Cu/CuSO4 half reaction ( reduction )
Cu2+(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s) ( E°: -0.340 V )

The net cell reaction is:
Cu2+(aq) + 2e- -> Cu(s) ( E°: -0.340 V )

A Daniell Cell consists of a large copper cylinder (anode) filled with a copper sulphate solution. In order to maintain the concentration of the copper sulphate solution copper sulphate crystals are placed at the bottom of the copper cylinder Inside the copper cylinder is a porous pot filled with a sulphuric acid solution. Finally a zinc rod (cathode) is placed inside the porous pot.

The reaction starts with the negatively charged sulphate ions inside the porous pot. The negative sulphate ions react with the zinc rod creating zinc sulphate. The zinc rod attains a negative charge with the excess of electrons created by the production of zinc sulphate. On the other hand the hydrogen ions move to the copper vessel. The free ions of the copper then neutralise the hydrogen ions to form hydrogen atoms. The copper cylinder (anode) becomes positively charged due to a deficiency of electrons. Finally the hydrogen atoms react with the copper sulphate solution to form sulphuric acid.


Sulphuric Acid dissolving:
H2SO4 -> 2H+ + SO4-2

Reaction at the cathode:
Zn + SO4 <=> ZnSO4 + 2e-

Reaction at the anode:
CuSO4 + 2H <=> H2SO4 + Cu

"Lead-Acid Batteries" - The first rechargeable batteries

The first lead-acid battery was invented in 1859 by Gaston Planté. Lead-acid batteries were revolutionary because they were the first to be rechargeable. This battery does not come without severe drawbacks. It has a very low energy to weight ratio and a low energy to volume ratio. It does however have a high power to weight ratio making it an attractive choice for use in starter motors. This inexpensive but specialised battery dominated the battery market, accounting for 45% of all battery sales in 1999.


Negative plate reaction
Pb(s) + HSO-4(aq) -> PbSO4(s) + H4(aq) + 2e4

Positive plate reaction
PbO2(s) + HSO-4(aq) + 3H+(aq) + 2e- -> PbSO4(s) + 2H2O(l)

The total reaction can be written as
Pb(s) + PbO2(s) + 2H2SO4(aq) -> 2PbSO4(s) + 2H2O(l) E० cell = 2.05V

Lead-acid batteries are relatively simple. They consist of one lead cathode and another lead(IV) oxide anode. The cathode and anode are put inside a container filled with sulphuric acid. As the pure lead cathode discharges it bonds with a sulphate ion creating lead sulphate. As lead(IV) oxide cathode receives electrons it reacts with the sulphuric acid and the hydrogen to create lead sulphate and water.

Because of the nature of their chemistry a lead acid battery has a lead cathode and lead(IV) oxide anode when it is charged. When the battery is discharged it has two lead sulphate rods and a less concentrated sulphuric acid solution. Measuring the concentration of the sulphuric acid solution allows the battery level to be determined.

"Lithium-ion batteries" - A modern wonder

Lithium ion batteries see a large majority presence in electronics, vehicles, and aerospace technology. They are popular in these categories because they are rechargeable and have a high energy to volume, and energy to weight ratio. Pure lithium is extremely reactive, which is why lithium-ion batteries don’t use pure lithium. Instead, lithium-ion batteries contain a lithium-metal oxide, such as lithium-cobalt oxide.


Lithium-doped cobalt oxide half reaction (cathode)
CoO2 + Li+ + e- <=> LiCoO2

Graphite half reaction (anode)
LiC6 <=> C6 + Li + + e-

The net cell reaction is:
LiC6 + CoO2 <=> C6 + LiCoO2

Lithium ion batteries advance our understanding of batteries taking on a completely new structure. In place of a container with electrodes lithium ion batteries resemble a series of sheets.

A lithium ion battery consists of three main sheets. The first being a copper sheet (anode) and the second being an organic salt of lithium as the electrolyte. The third sheet is an aluminum sheet (cathode). A lithium metal oxide is pasted to the cathode and graphite is pasted to the anode. To charge the battery a power source is connected to the battery. The electrons are pulled away from the lithium atoms and into the cathode. The lithium ions are attracted to the anode and rest in between graphite layers. Once all the lithium ions are stored in the graphite, the battery is fully charged.

Once the power source is removed and a load is connected to the batteries the lithium ions will want to return to their stable state. The lithium ions return to the metal oxide and the electrons return through the wires connecting the cathode and anode. Once all the lithium ions have returned to the metal oxide the battery is depleted.


Over Discharging supersaturated lithium cobalt oxide, leads to the production of lithium oxide:
Li+ + e- + LiCoO2 -> Li2O + CoO

Overcharging up to 5.2 volts leads to the synthesis of cobalt(IV) oxide:
LiCoO2 -> Li+ + CoO2 + e-
